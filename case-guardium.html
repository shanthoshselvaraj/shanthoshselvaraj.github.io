The Brief

Add Excel-like features to the tool.

A director noticed something in a routine user conversation. Security analysts were spending significant time in Excel alongside IBM Guardium DDR. The ask was simple: could someone look into it and make the Excel handoff smoother? Maybe some scripting support. A quality-of-life improvement.

I was brought in to scope and deliver that. I did not deliver that.

Why Analysts Went to Excel

A tool that made investigation impossible without leaving it.

A security analyst's job is straightforward: something suspicious happened on the network, and they need to decide if it was a real attack or a false alarm. IBM Guardium DDR is the tool built for exactly that. To understand more, let's experience a day as an analyst.

The tool had every signal. Fragmented across four screens, three apps, and machine-generated descriptions that meant nothing. All of it raw, disconnected, and meaningless until an analyst exported it to Excel and rebuilt the story by hand. The tool had made itself optional.

The Design

Show the story. Not the data.

One view. One narrative thread. A verdict reached without leaving the page.

1

The tool already had the answer. It just wouldn't connect the dots.

Every signal existed inside DDR. It just never connected them. The redesign didn't add features, it changed what the tool believed its job was.

2

A risk event is not a snapshot. It is a story that unfolds.

What starts as a minor anomaly can quietly escalate. The investigation opens to a timeline that shows that progression as one connected thread, not isolated rows. The three reports that previously lived in a separate application sit at the bottom of the same view, pre-filtered for the incident. Everything the analyst needs, before they ask.

Resistance

Four walls. Four different arguments.

1

The distributed team: "It is already working"

The global DDR product team pushed back immediately. The tool had existing users, existing workflows, a functioning product. There was no broken metric to point to. From their position, adding features was the brief. Questioning the fundamental design was not.

2

The original design team: "It is their muscle memory"

The previous designers argued that DDR's users were power users, day-in day-out operators who had internalised the interaction patterns. Changing those patterns would break their flow regardless of whether the new design was objectively better. Familiarity was being defended as a feature.

3

Engineering: "It cannot be built"

The development team said the redesign was not technically feasible as scoped. Loading the investigation data and pre-filtered reports into a single view would cause performance issues. The visual synthesis layer was not something they could ship at the required scale.

4

Senior stakeholder: "I cannot take that risk"

When I proposed showing the prototype to existing customers for validation, a senior stakeholder refused. If customers saw a redesign concept and did not like it, IBM's relationship with those accounts was at stake. Testing with the people who knew the product best was deemed too dangerous.

The Move

If I cannot test with customers, I will prove it without them first.

I built the prototype regardless. Then I went to the research team and asked for a general pool of security professionals who were not IBM Guardium customers. Same user type, same domain expertise, zero relationship risk. The senior stakeholder could not object to that.

The prototype tested well. Users understood the timeline view immediately. They found the visual data representations faster than text tables. They did not need to be taught how to navigate, because there was almost nothing to navigate.

I took the validated results to the director and asked for the customer presentation. Not as a concept. As a validated design with research backing. The ask was different now, and the director agreed.

The actual customers were shown the redesign. Their response was the moment that answered every argument in the room: "We did not know we needed this. This makes our lives so much easier."

That single sentence, from existing customers who had been using the old tool for years, answered the muscle memory argument, the risk argument, and the "it is already working" argument in one go.
