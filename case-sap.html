What If the "S" in ESG Actually Meant Something?

In 2015, the UN set 17 Sustainable Development Goals. Companies worldwide adopted them, committing to decent work, wellbeing, reduced inequalities. Ten years on, the E in ESG has carbon dashboards and verified frameworks. The S has no equivalent. No verified methodology. No global benchmark. The category is completely open.

How I Got Here

A competition. A ping. An invitation to think.

In 2020, I represented my team at SAP Innvent, an internal entrepreneurship challenge. We placed top 3 out of 80. I presented the problem-solving portion personally.

Weeks later, a message from a senior team: "We're looking at a problem. Can we get your thoughts?"

That became a rotation in SAP's CEO Office innovation team. These are groups that exist to pursue ideas that don't fit inside any existing product line. My job wasn't to design a product. It was to help determine whether a hypothesis was worth betting on.

The Provocation

Every company tracks carbon. Nobody tracks people.

Companies globally have committed to UN Sustainable Development Goals, picking relevant goals and reporting progress through ESG frameworks. The E pillar has carbon dashboards, emissions targets, verified accounting standards. The S has no equivalent. No verified methodology. No global benchmark. The category is completely open.

Whoever defines how the S gets measured owns that methodology as IP. If SAP builds the framework and gets it adopted by large enterprises, then by governments, every organisation that wants to benchmark against the global standard needs SAP's tools. That's not a product feature. That's a category SAP could own outright, the way FICO owns credit scoring.

The brand implication is equally significant. SAP is known as an ERP company. Being the organisation that solved people sustainability at a global standard level is a completely different story, one that puts SAP alongside UN agencies rather than in the enterprise software vendor category. The data that comes with global adoption, anonymised and aggregated people signals across thousands of organisations, is an intelligence asset nobody else on earth would have.

The question wasn't "how do we improve people reporting." It was: what if the S in ESG produced genuine organisational insight? Would companies invest in it? Would governments mandate it?

This innovation project was the test: was the problem real enough, and the insight valuable enough, that organisations and governments would actually adopt a framework built around it?

The Model

Don't add a new survey. Connect what already exists.

The design decision: no new data collection. Sick leave logs, benefits utilisation, manager action completion rates, communication reach, engagement patterns. It was all there. The work was building a cause-and-effect model that connected these signals into something readable in real time, not annually.

Sick leave frequency and stated reasons fed into an ergonomics and wellbeing indicator. Benefits awareness gaps, measured against communication outputs, surfaced communication failures rather than policy failures. Manager action completion tracked against team wellbeing over time, making accountability visible rather than theoretical.

The output: a People Sustainability Score sitting alongside carbon credits in the existing sustainability dashboard. Managers owned it. Alerts fired when it dropped. An organisational goal tracked it company-wide, the same way carbon targets are tracked today.

The hardest design challenge wasn't what to measure. It was cultural portability. A declining score means different things in Germany and India. We built interpretive context into the thresholds so the model didn't flatten human difference into a single comparable number.

Why SAP Bet On This

This wasn't goodwill. It was a category-defining opportunity.

Carbon measurement has the Gold Standard, Verra, ISO frameworks. Verified, globally referenced, legally cited by regulators and investors. The S has no equivalent. No verified methodology. No global benchmark. The category is completely open.

Whoever defines how the S gets measured owns that methodology as IP. If SAP builds the framework and gets it adopted by large enterprises, then by governments, every organisation that wants to benchmark against the global standard needs SAP's tools. That's not a product feature. That's a category SAP could own outright, the way FICO owns credit scoring.

The brand implication is equally significant. SAP is known as an ERP company. Being the organisation that solved people sustainability at a global standard level is a completely different story, one that puts SAP alongside UN agencies rather than in the enterprise software vendor category. The data that comes with global adoption, anonymised and aggregated people signals across thousands of organisations, is an intelligence asset nobody else on earth would have.

SAP had acquired Qualtrics, the employee experience platform used by most large enterprises, in 2019. The data infrastructure to run a people sustainability framework at scale already existed inside SAP's ecosystem. Regulatory tailwinds from EU CSRD were also expanding mandatory ESG reporting to include social metrics. The conditions were unusually aligned.

This innovation project was the test: was the problem real enough, and the insight valuable enough, that organisations and governments would actually adopt a framework built around it?

Validation

The hypothesis held.

Three major enterprise partners engaged with the framework directly: two global OEMs and a large technology company, all SAP customers. One was building a parallel initiative and saw the model as closely aligned with what they were trying to do. These weren't courtesy conversations. They were organisations asking how to use it.

The work reached government level. I was present in a meeting where the framework was presented to Germany's Social Affairs Minister. I didn't speak. My German wasn't good enough. But the nature of that conversation was its own proof: a government was already thinking about how to mandate and measure people sustainability, and SAP's framework was in the room as a potential answer.

That's what the innovation project was ultimately testing: not whether the product worked, but whether the problem was real enough that the world would come to SAP to solve it. The answer, at that point, was yes.
