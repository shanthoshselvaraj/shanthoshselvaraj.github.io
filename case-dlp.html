How it started

The brief was simple. The question wasn't.

IBM had no DLP product. Microsoft Purview was already the default. Startups were shipping faster than any enterprise vendor could respond. The window was narrowing. The brief: build one and take share.

I had one question before anything else.

Security teams don't switch DLP tools. The category becomes infrastructure. Nobody replaces infrastructure unless something is broken at the foundation. The question wasn't how to build faster. It was whether the category itself was broken and whether IBM could build against that break instead of into the noise.

I directed the research team to find out. Not what security teams wanted from a tool. What the market had systematically gotten wrong.

Why Existing Tools Fail

The threat moved. The tools didn't

Traditional DLP is built on policies. Rules written by hand, designed around threats someone anticipated. That assumption held for a decade.

Then AI made it irrelevant overnight.

THE ASSUMPTION
Defending against known break-ins.
External Threats
Known Shares
THE REALITY
Data leaking through untracked workflows.
Web Browsers
Native Apps
CLI Prompts
VSCode Extensions

This is not an execution problem. It is a structural one.

The tools didn't fail. They worked exactly as designed. In response, the industry added more rules, more specialists, more alerts. The average SOC team now receives over 4,000 alerts a day. Two thirds go uninvestigated. More than half of the rest are false positives. False positives are not the problem, they are the symptom of a system enforcing rigid rules on behaviour that was never rigid to begin with.

Every competitor was building faster versions of the same broken system. I didn't.

The Bet

What if I made the villain the hero?

Not AI-assisted. Not AI-featured. Fully agentic.

The same AI creating the problem was also the only thing fast enough to catch it.

That was the bet. Not AI-assisted. Not AI-featured. Fully agentic, where intelligence drives the entire experience from the moment you log in. IBM had never built anything like this in its security portfolio. The resistance wasn't "it can't be done".  It was "a known formula might work, why take the risk".  That sentence, spoken inside one of the largest enterprise software companies in the world, is exactly the kind of thinking that lets a category stay broken for a decade.

During early exploration I found IBM already had a data pipeline product in the portfolio. A pipe with visibility but no judgment. I absorbed it as a native layer for the agents' baseline. They understood the organisation's data environment before a single analyst logged in. The harder call made the platform possible.

Every other product was designed for threats someone had already named. This one was built for everything they hadn't.

What I Built

Four systems. One premise.

The agentic layer wasn't one thing. It was four systems, each one built around the same premise: the bottleneck the category had accepted as permanent was the one worth removing.

1

The dashboard that doesn't exist until you log in

Every IBM security tool shipped with the same static dashboard. Every sales pitch promised this one would be different. Not one of them was.

A CISO and a data analyst see completely different interfaces without configuring anything. When a phishing campaign hits, the CISO clicks one card and instantly shares a live dashboard with the SOC team.

ibm-dlp.security/dashboard

The interface the room saw. No template. No configuration.

2

Policies that maintain themselves

The shift was to remove humans from detection entirely: agents observe behavior directly, and when compliance needs policies, the platform writes them itself. Thousands that nobody could manage become hundreds that manage themselves.

4

Onboarding that doesn't need a support call

Not a setup wizard. Not a chatbot. The onboarding agent works like an after-sales engineer and a domain consultant in the same room. It knows what to ask, knows when to act, and knows when to step back. In beta, support calls dropped by 80%. That number came before anyone expected it.

The Room

It had been called a fairy tale for months.

I built it anyway. Then I put it in a room with IBM's Security CTO, the VP, the design director, and the dev director.

I logged in. The dashboard assembled itself in real time, tuned for the persona in front of them. No configuration. No template. No admin. The room had walked in skeptical. It went quiet.

The VP turned to the dev director:
"We are making your life hard and I love it."

Then to me:
"I wasn't expecting this."

The CTO leaned forward. He didn't stop at the dashboard. He wanted to see the policy engine, the incident view, the full agentic layer underneath. Then he looked up.

"Shanthosh, unknowingly you might have solved some of IBM's longest standing problems."

That sentence came from the CTO of IBM Security. In a room where the same idea had been called a fairy tale six months earlier. He opened the doors. IBM WatsonX Orchestrate expressed interest in orchestrating the platform's agents. What had started as a product nobody believed in was now an IBM-wide architecture conversation.
